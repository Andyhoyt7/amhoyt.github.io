about

Andrew Hoyt is a PhD candidate in the history of science at Princeton University with interests in the twentieth-century life sciences, environmental and political history, and North American Indigenous history. His dissertation project, Chaos Ecology: Science and Governance after Nature, examines the rise of the "new ecology" in the final decades of the twentieth century. Departing from earlier conceptions of ecology as ordered and predictable, this scientific movement sought to redefine nature as messy, contingent, and constantly in flux. Hoyt's project traces new ecological ideas from their midcentury scientific and political origins to their lasting consequences for historical, social scientific, and postcolonial thought.

Hoyt's other projects explore the relationship between conceptions of natural order and governance in a range of contexts. These include research on the politics of Ojibwe resource management, the development of the field of resource economics, and the shifting political economy of land management in the Midwest. His M.A. thesis examined the emergence of ecological restoration as a concept, research agenda, and set of practices, with particular attention to the dominant Midwestern grasses of corn, prairie tallgrass, and wild rice.

Before starting at Princeton, Andrew graduated from Carleton College with a B.A. in History and a background in ecological research. He went on to work as an energy analyst and climate policy advocate in Minneapolis for several years before completing an M.A. in History at Trent University in Ontario.
