I am a PhD candidate in the history of science at Princeton University. My research interests span the history of the life and social sciences, environmental and political history, and North American Indigenous history, but revolve around a central question: how have ideas about natural order and governance influenced one another since the mid-twentieth century?

Through my M.A. thesis, I considered this question by tracing the rise of ecological restoration as a concept, research agenda, and set of management interventions in the Midwestern United States. The project focused especially on the region's characteristic grasses — prairie tallgrass, corn, and wild rice — and the conflicting science and politics in which they have been emboroiled since the early twentieth century. More recently, I’ve been writing about Ojibwe relational restoration as a form of world-making and the political and ecological ideals behind changing public land management regimes in the Corn Belt.

Before starting at Princeton, I graduated from Carleton College with a B.A. in History, a minor in Medieval and Renaissance Studies, and a background in ecological research. I went on to work as an energy analyst and climate policy advocate in Minneapolis for several years before completing an M.A. in History at Trent University in Ontario.
